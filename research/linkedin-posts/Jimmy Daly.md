My first real project at Miro was to put together a presentation for one of our execs. His words, his ideas. I facilitated the brain dump, wrapped it in the right narrative, then packaged it in the right format.

It went extremely well and I wanted to unpack a few things I was reminded of in doing it:

1️⃣ The classic content problem is that the people with the most to say don't have the time to say it. Helping them turn their own ideas into words is very valuable.

2️⃣ Turning a brain dump into a narrative is a content skill that will never go out of style. And by doing it, you actually help the CxO you're working with find clarity too. They have the expertise and insights, but may not have the time to parse them into artifacts that become useful for marketing.

3️⃣ As I wrote on the Animalz blog many years ago, "Unlocking internal knowledge is actually a great content strategy." Especially right now when I believe ideas are more powerful than scale.

4️⃣ Good content travels far and wide. This presentation landed not because the slides were great, but because we turned a batch of related ideas into a story that's easy to tell. It's been passed around the company and even used by other senior leaders since. The deliverable was a slide deck, but the value is the story.

5️⃣ Plant a flag! We took an abstract concept, drew a clear line around it and gave it a name. Now we have our own language to describe a pattern that all of our customers are experiencing but haven't had the short-hand to make it easy to discuss.

I've been reading Ramp's Economic Lab for a while now. It's interesting and I also think it's a *great* content play. 

Ramp is sitting on a unique dataset. They of course know this and hired an economist (s/o Ara Kharazian!) to analyze and write about trends. Of note, they publish on Substack, likely to free Ara up from any constraints that normal content is subject to. To make this work, Ara needs the freedom to use Substack as readers intend, write in the first person, have real opinions, etc.

Today, I saw Ara's recent post about Claude beating OpenAI for business AI adoption. Just an hour or two later, the Wall Street Journal published a post on the same topic, citing Ara and Ramp as the source of the data.

There are so many things to like about this:

* Identifying how useful the data is and acting on it. I'm assuming it was a pretty huge effort to clean this data up, get legal approval, etc.
* Instead of benchmark reports, they chose one person and a Substack account to publish and distribute. A good example of meeting your audience where they are.
* Giving this content some breathing room and not plastering it with CTAs for a trial or a webinar
* Empowering Ara to build his OWN personal brand as he grows Ramp's presence. (In my experience, most companies are afraid of this unless it's the founder/CEO.)

I have no affiliation toRamp but just love this strategy. Check out the Substack here: https://lnkd.in/gqq2jBFB
